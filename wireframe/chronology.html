— Ch. 02 / Life

A living laboratory
for island
conservation.

Ten species of seabird nest on Tetiaroa's motu. Green sea turtles (honu) return to the same sand where they hatched — up to ten nests, 60–110 eggs at a time. Blacktip reef sharks patrol the lagoon. The world's largest land arthropod, the coconut crab, lives beneath the palms.

For a century, introduced rats ate the eggs and pulled the whole food web down with them. In 2022, a multi-year eradication across 520 hectares removed them. This is the inventory we now keep.

— Ch. 03 / Work

A lab that doesn't
look like a lab.

Long-term monitoring. Rat-free seabird recovery. Mosquito elimination without insecticide. The Ecostation on Motu Onetahi — Te Fare rua ihi, the House of Multiple Sciences — hosts researchers from 22 institutions on 14 concurrent projects.

A quiet kind of science: patient, local, and unafraid of slow timescales. Seabird transects every quarter. Turtle nests mapped nightly, October through March. Reef surveys annually. Data is open; results are published; methods travel.

We call it a living laboratory because the atoll is both the question and the answer.

— a slow chronology
1967
Marlon Brando acquires Tetiaroa with the intent of keeping it whole.
2010
Tetiaroa Society founded as a 501(c)(3) dedicated to the atoll.
2014
Ecostation opens on Motu Onetahi — the House of Multiple Sciences.
2018
TARP begins: multi-year baseline across every habitat on the atoll.
2022
Rat eradication completed across 520 hectares — largest of its kind in FP.
2026
Seabird recovery year 4 published; mosquito elimination scales atoll-wide.
